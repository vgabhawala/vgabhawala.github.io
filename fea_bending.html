Credit Card Bending Machine Design

Project Timeline: November 2024 – December 2024

This project involved designing a custom credit card bending machine to replicate and test the failure conditions experienced by physical cards during repeated flexing. The setup consisted of a card holder, linear actuator with clamp, load cell, and base plate, all laid out for precise alignment and controlled force application. The user inserts the card into the slot of the card holder, after which the linear actuator is positioned to match the slot height. A custom clamp is then attached to the exposed portion of the card, allowing the actuator to apply a repeatable bending force.

Components

  • 1× Custom Actuator
  • 1× Custom Cross Head
  • Polyurethane Clamp Surface
  • M5 Screws and Welding Material

Defining the Variables

F = Point Force on Credit Card
σy = Yield Strength of 316L Stainless Steel
I = Moment of Inertia of Credit Card
L = Length of Uninserted Part of the Credit Card
t = Thickness of Credit Card

Iₓₓ = (b * h³) / 12 Iₓₓ = 2.304 × 10⁻¹² m⁴

Bending stress can be calculated from the equation:

σ = M·y / I M = F·L y = t / 2

Hence,

F = (2·σ·I) / (t·L)

Using:

  • σy = 205 MPa
  • b = 54 mm
  • L = 42.8 mm
  • t = 0.8 mm

We calculate:

F = 27.588 N

Finite Element Analysis of Credit Card

To evaluate bending performance, Finite Element Analysis (FEA) was conducted on both a traditional PVC credit card and a titanium metal card under identical loading conditions. The PVC card showed significant deformation with a maximum von Mises stress of approximately 190 MPa, concentrated near the fixed edge. In contrast, the titanium card exhibited far lower deflection and distributed stress due to its higher modulus of elasticity and yield strength, confirming its superior structural resilience.
