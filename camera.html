Underwater Camera System

Project Timeline: August 2023 – May 2024

The National Aquarium opened a Harbor Wetlands exhibit to recreate Baltimore’s historical salt marshes. To educate visitors about life under water, the aquarium asked our team to design an interactive exhibit that offers an immersive underwater view of the Harbor Wetlands.

Background

I led a team of 2 Mechanical Engineers and 2 Electrical Engineers.

Our final design involved an underwater camera system divided into two parts. The “above water” component described the display and the interactive elements that visitors would control and physically interact with. The “underwater” component described the camera, enclosure, and structural elements that would capture and send imagery to the above water display.

Ideation

Figure 4: Concept sketches showing the internal and external components of an underwater camera unit.

Figure 5. We designed and tested a waterproof cylindrical (PVC) camera enclosure to ensure image clarity and water resistance in saltwater conditions. The cylindrical design used a 4” PVC tube with a sealed end cap, a TPU adapter with O-rings, and a transparent polymer window secured with corrosion-resistant hardware. Marine epoxy was applied for added sealing. Each enclosure was submerged in the harbor with internal cameras to evaluate image quality and waterproofing performance.

We designed an underwater camera system using a clear cast acrylic tube (11.8” length, 4” ID, 4.5” OD) sealed with anodized aluminum end caps. The bottom cap is solid, while the top cap features five openings—four sealed with pressure relief valves and one fitted with a WetLink Penetrator for a waterproof wire pass-through. The enclosure houses a 360° Ricoh Theta V camera and is secured to a stable, table-like structure using a custom 3D-printed ABS clamp. This structure clips into the fibergrate base of the wetland channel to prevent movement.

Figure 6: Finalized CAD design in image on the left and WetLink Penetrator on the right.
Figure 7. To maximize underwater FOV and minimize enclosure occlusion, we developed a 3D-printed mount for the camera. The lower disk houses a FingerBot to toggle power on a schedule via the Smart Life app (Bluetooth).

Final Design

To prototype the interactive display, we built a wooden frame using 2×4s and a TV mount, focusing on user testing to determine ideal viewing dimensions. The frame included an adjustable-height table and side/top plywood panels to reduce glare. A joystick was mounted in a position that mimicked its final intended placement.

On the software side, we used OpenCV to process and display 360° camera footage on a 2D monitor, initially prototyping with sample footage and live webcam input. The joystick served as the control interface for navigating the display.
