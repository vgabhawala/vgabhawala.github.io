Underwater Camera System

Project Timeline: August 2023 – May 2024

The National Aquarium opened a Harbor Wetlands exhibit to recreate Baltimore’s historical salt marshes. To educate visitors about life under water, the aquarium asked our team to design an interactive exhibit that offers an immersive underwater view of the Harbor Wetlands.

Background

I led a team of 2 Mechanical Engineers and 2 Electrical Engineers.

Our final design involved an underwater camera system divided into two parts. The “above water” component described the display and the interactive elements that visitors would control and physically interact with. The “underwater” component described the camera, enclosure, and structural elements that would capture and send imagery to the above water display.

Ideation

Figure 1. Concept sketches showing the internal and external components of an underwater camera unit.

Figure 2. We designed and tested a waterproof cylindrical (PVC) camera enclosure to ensure image clarity and water resistance in saltwater conditions. The cylindrical design used a 4” PVC tube with a sealed end cap, a TPU adapter with O-rings, and a transparent polymer window secured with corrosion-resistant hardware. Marine epoxy was applied for added sealing. Each enclosure was submerged in the harbor with internal cameras to evaluate image quality and waterproofing performance.

In order to properly position the camera within the capsule (i.e., maximize the view of the underwater while minimizing the view of the capsule, endcaps, supports, etc.) we developed a 3D printed mount that the camera rests upon inside the capsule. The bottom disk houses a spot to place the FingerBot, a bluetooth button presser that can be scheduled via the Smart Life app.

Final Design

To prototype the interactive display, we built a wooden frame using 2×4s and a TV mount, focusing on user testing to determine ideal viewing dimensions. The frame included an adjustable-height table and side/top plywood panels to reduce glare. A joystick was mounted in a position that mimicked its final intended placement.

On the software side, we used OpenCV to process and display 360° camera footage on a 2D monitor, initially prototyping with sample footage and live webcam input. The joystick served as the control interface for navigating the display.
